Low Country Dance

Low Country dance, or Pahatha Rata Natum, is a Sri Lankan dance style from the southern coastal lowlands, characterized by its ritualistic and ceremonial nature. It is known for the use of elaborate masks and is performed to appease evil spirits and deities, as seen in rituals like the Devil Dance (Thovil) and the Daha Ata Sanniya, which aims to cure 18 different diseases.

Originating from southern coastal regions, Low Country dances are ritualistic performances intended to drive away evil spirits. Dancers wear masks and vibrant costumes accompanied by traditional drumming.

These dances are highly ritualistic in nature, primarily performed to appease malevolent spirits, promote healing, and seek blessings from gods and goddesses. They serve both spiritual and therapeutic purposes within the communities that practice them.

The tradition of these dances originates from the southern coastal regions of Sri Lanka, including areas such as Galle, Matara, and Hambantota. Over time, they have become an integral part of the cultural heritage of these regions.

A distinctive element of these dances is the use of elaborate masks representing various characters, including demons, birds, and reptiles. Among the most notable are the Gurulu Raksha, known as the king of birds, and the Naga Raksha, regarded as the king of cobras. These masks play a central role in conveying the story and symbolism of each performance.

Rituals form an essential component of the performances. Key ceremonies include the Devil Dance (Thovil) and Shanthikarma rituals, which are intended for purification. Examples include the Daha Ata Sanniya, which addresses the 18 types of sickness, and the Kolam, a more playful and entertaining form of dance.

The performances are accompanied by specific musical elements, most prominently the ‘Yukk’ drum. Dancers often enhance the rhythm by using halved coconut shells, adding a unique percussive sound that complements the movements and the overall ritualistic atmosphere.
